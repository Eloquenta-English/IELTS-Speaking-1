Technology & Social Media

IELTS Speaking — B1

Practice all three parts

Question 1
How much time do you spend on the internet each day?
Model Answer:
I'd say I spend about four to five hours a day online, which is probably more than I should. Most of that is split between social media, watching videos, and doing schoolwork. I try to limit my screen time in the evening, but it's hard when so much of my social life happens on my phone.
Question 2
What do you usually do online?
Model Answer:
Mainly I use social media — Instagram and TikTok mostly — to keep up with friends and see what's happening. I also watch a lot of YouTube videos, especially about technology and gaming. For school, I use online resources like Khan Academy and Google Docs for group projects. Occasionally I play online games with friends too.
Question 3
Do you use social media? Which platforms?
Model Answer:
Yes, I'm quite active on social media. I use Instagram the most — mostly for sharing photos and stories. I also use TikTok for short videos, though I tend to watch more than I create. I have a Snapchat account too, which I use for quick messages with close friends. I don't really use Facebook — that's more for my parents' generation.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future. Don't memorise scripted answers — examiners can tell.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

An App You Use Regularly

Describe an app you use regularly. You should say:

  • What the app is
  • How often you use it
  • What you use it for
  • Why you find it useful
Model Answer:
An app I use regularly is called Duolingo — it's a language-learning app that helps you learn new languages through short, gamified lessons. I use it almost every day, usually for about 15-20 minutes in the evening or on my commute to school.

I'm currently learning Italian on it, and I use it to practise vocabulary, grammar, and listening skills. The lessons are broken down into small chunks, so it doesn't feel overwhelming, and there are streaks and rewards that keep you motivated.

I find it really useful because it makes learning feel like a game rather than a chore. The bite-sized lessons fit easily into my daily routine, and I can see my progress over time, which is really satisfying. It's not a replacement for real conversation practice, but it's a great supplement.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions related to the Part 2 topic.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view. Use phrases like "On one hand... on the other hand..." or "Some people argue that... but I believe..."

Question 1
Do you think people spend too much time on social media?
Model Answer:
Yes, I think many people — especially teenagers — spend far too much time scrolling through social media feeds. Studies show that the average person spends over two hours a day on social platforms, and I think I'm probably above that average. The problem is that social media is designed to be addictive — it triggers dopamine hits that keep you coming back. I think it's important to be conscious of how much time you're spending and take regular breaks.
Question 2
How has technology changed the way people communicate?
Model Answer:
Technology has completely revolutionised communication. Twenty years ago, you'd write letters or make phone calls — now we have instant messaging, video calls, and social media platforms that let you connect with anyone in the world instantly. I think the biggest change is that communication has become more casual and frequent but also more superficial. We send dozens of short messages a day but rarely have deep, meaningful conversations. On the positive side, technology has made it possible to maintain relationships with people who live far away, which is wonderful.
Question 3
Is it a good thing that children use technology from a young age?
Model Answer:
I think it's a double-edged sword. On one hand, children today have access to incredible educational resources — they can learn any skill, explore any subject, and connect with people from around the world. Technology can be a powerful tool for creativity and learning. On the other hand, there are real concerns about screen time affecting children's attention spans, social skills, and mental health. I think the key is balance and parental guidance — children should use technology, but with limits and supervision.
