Business & Management

IELTS Speaking — C1

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
What qualities make a good leader?
Model Answer:
I think emotional intelligence is probably the most important quality. A good leader needs to understand and motivate people, not just give orders. Adaptability is also crucial — the business environment changes so rapidly that rigid thinking can be fatal.
Question 2
Do you think people are born with leadership skills or can they be learned?
Model Answer:
I think it's a combination, but I lean towards learned skills. Some people may have natural confidence, but real leadership requires developed skills like communication, strategic thinking, and empathy. These can all be cultivated through experience and deliberate practice.
Question 3
How has the role of managers changed in recent years?
Model Answer:
The shift has been enormous. Managers used to primarily direct and control workers. Now, the emphasis is on coaching, facilitating, and empowering teams. The rise of knowledge work means you can't just tell people what to do — you need to create environments where people can do their best work.
Question 4
What do you think will be the biggest challenge for businesses in the coming decade?
Model Answer:
I think managing the transition to sustainable practices will be the biggest challenge. Companies need to fundamentally rethink their operations while remaining profitable. Those that manage this transition well will thrive, and those that don't will struggle to attract customers and talent.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Business Decision

You should say:

  • What the decision was
  • Why you had to make it
  • What the alternatives were
  • And explain what the result was
Model Answer:
A significant business decision I had to make was whether to invest heavily in developing a new product line or to focus on improving our existing offerings. This was about three years ago when I was working as a product manager at a medium-sized tech company. The dilemma was that our current products were profitable but showing signs of stagnation. The new product line was risky — it required significant investment and there was no guarantee of market acceptance. After analysing market data and consulting with the team, I decided to invest in the new line while maintaining the existing products with a smaller team. The result was that after 18 months, the new product line accounted for 30% of our revenue. It was a difficult decision at the time, but it paid off. The key lesson I learned was the importance of data-driven decision making and having the courage to take calculated risks.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view.

Question 1
Do you think large corporations have too much power?
Model Answer:
I think in some sectors, yes. When a few companies control most of the market, they can effectively set prices and influence regulations. I think stronger antitrust enforcement would help. But I also think large corporations bring benefits — economies of scale, R&D investment, and global reach. It's about finding the right balance.
Question 2
Should executives be paid multiples of what their lowest-paid workers earn?
Model Answer:
I think the current multiples are excessive. When a CEO earns 300 times what their lowest-paid worker earns, it creates resentment and undermines company culture. I think a reasonable multiple — perhaps 20-30 times — would be more justifiable. Ultimately, a company's success depends on contributions at every level.
Question 3
How do you think remote work will affect management in the future?
Model Answer:
I think it will require a fundamental shift in management philosophy. You can't micromanage people you can't see. Managers will need to focus on outcomes rather than hours worked. I think this will actually be positive — it forces trust and autonomy. But it will also require better communication skills and more intentional team building.
Question 4
Do you think businesses should prioritise profit or social responsibility?
Model Answer:
I think this is a false dichotomy. The most successful businesses I've seen do both. Increasingly, consumers and employees expect companies to have a social purpose. I think businesses that ignore social responsibility will struggle to attract talent and customers in the long run, even if they're profitable in the short term.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers with specific examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use chronological structure and all cue card prompts
  • ✓ Part 3: Discuss multiple perspectives before stating your position
  • ✓ C1 tip: Use sophisticated, nuanced language with hedging and qualification
