Friends & Family

IELTS Speaking — B1

Practice all three parts

Question 1
Do you spend a lot of time with your family?
Model Answer:
Yes, I try to spend as much time as possible with my family. We have dinner together almost every evening, and on weekends we often go out for walks or visit relatives. My parents are both quite busy during the week, so those weekend moments together are really important to me.
Question 2
Who are you most similar to in your family?
Model Answer:
People say I'm most similar to my mother. We both have the same sense of humour, we're both quite stubborn, and we share an interest in reading and cooking. But I also have some traits from my father — he's very calm and patient, and I try to be like that, especially when things get stressful.
Question 3
How often do you see your friends?
Model Answer:
I see my close friends quite often — we're in the same class at school, so we see each other every weekday. On weekends, we usually meet up in the city centre, go to a cafe, or play football in the park. During holidays, of course, it's harder because some people go away, but we stay in touch through group chats.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future. Don't memorise scripted answers — examiners can tell.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Good Friend

Describe a good friend. You should say:

  • Who this person is
  • How long you have known them
  • What you do together
  • Why this person is important to you
Model Answer:
I'd like to talk about my best friend, Lukas. We've known each other since primary school — that's about nine years now. We became friends because we sat next to each other in class and discovered we both loved the same video game.

We do a lot of things together. We play football every Saturday morning, we study for exams together, and we often just hang out at each other's houses watching movies or playing games. We also go on bike rides along the Danube in summer.

The reason he's so important to me is that I can tell him absolutely anything. He's never judged me, and he's always been there when I've had a difficult time — like when I was struggling at school or when my parents were going through a rough patch. That kind of trust is rare and I really value it.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions related to the Part 2 topic.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view. Use phrases like "On one hand... on the other hand..." or "Some people argue that... but I believe..."

Question 1
Why do some people prefer to spend time alone rather than in a group?
Model Answer:
I think it comes down to personality. Some people are naturally introverted — they find social interaction draining rather than energising. For them, spending time alone is a way to recharge. Also, some people simply enjoy their own company and find that they can think more clearly or be more productive when they're by themselves. There's nothing wrong with that — it's just a different way of being.
Question 2
Do you think families are as close as they used to be?
Model Answer:
I think it varies a lot. In some ways, families today are closer because technology makes it easier to stay in touch — you can video call relatives who live far away, and family group chats keep everyone connected. But in other ways, I think modern life has made it harder to be close. People are busier, children have more activities and distractions, and everyone spends more time on their own devices. In Austria, family meals are still quite traditional, but I'm not sure that will last forever.
Question 3
How important do you think friendships are compared to family relationships?
Model Answer:
I think both are essential, but in different ways. Family is your foundation — they're the people who will always be there for you no matter what, and they provide stability and unconditional love. Friendships, on the other hand, are chosen relationships. Friends understand you in a way that family sometimes can't, because they're your age and share your interests. I think the ideal is to have both: a supportive family and a close group of friends. They complement each other.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers — give reasons + examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use all cue card prompts, structure chronologically
  • ✓ Part 3: Balance both sides before giving your opinion
  • ✓ Golden rule: Be specific and personal — avoid generic answers
