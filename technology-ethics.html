Technology Ethics

IELTS Speaking — C1

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
Do you think technology is making society more or less equal?
Model Answer:
I think it's doing both. Technology has democratised access to information and opportunity in remarkable ways. Someone in a developing country can now access the same educational resources as someone at Oxford. But technology also creates new inequalities — the digital divide, algorithmic bias, and the concentration of power in tech companies.
Question 2
Should there be limits on how much data companies can collect about users?
Model Answer:
Absolutely. I think the current situation, where companies know intimate details about people's lives, is unacceptable. I think users should have much more control over their data, and companies should be required to be transparent about what they collect and how they use it. Strong data protection regulation is essential.
Question 3
Do you think artificial intelligence will create more jobs than it destroys?
Model Answer:
I think in the long run, yes, but the transition will be painful. Historically, technological revolutions have ultimately created more jobs than they eliminated, but the new jobs often require different skills. The challenge is managing the transition — ensuring people can retrain and that the benefits are distributed fairly.
Question 4
How do you think social media has affected mental health?
Model Answer:
I think the evidence points to significant negative effects, particularly for young people. The constant comparison, the pressure to present a perfect life, and the addictive design of platforms all contribute to anxiety and depression. But I also think social media can be positive for people who find community and support online.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Technological Dilemma

You should say:

  • What the technology is
  • What benefits it offers
  • What risks or concerns it raises
  • And explain whether you think the benefits outweigh the risks
Model Answer:
A technological dilemma I find compelling is facial recognition technology. The benefits are significant — it can help find missing people, improve security, and streamline identification processes. But the risks are equally significant. It enables mass surveillance, can be used to track individuals without consent, and has been shown to have higher error rates for certain demographic groups. There are also concerns about consent — people are being scanned without their knowledge or permission. I think the risks outweigh the benefits in most applications. While there may be legitimate uses — like finding missing children — the potential for abuse is too great. I think we need strict regulation limiting when and how this technology can be used, and robust oversight to prevent misuse.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view.

Question 1
Should technology companies be held responsible for how their products are used?
Model Answer:
I think they have more responsibility than they currently accept. While users bear some responsibility for how they use tools, the companies that design the algorithms and platforms have enormous influence. I think we need a framework where companies are accountable for foreseeable harms, even if they're not directly causing them.
Question 2
Do you think people will become more or less dependent on technology in the future?
Model Answer:
I think we'll become more dependent, but I hope in more beneficial ways. The key is ensuring that technology serves human flourishing rather than just engagement and profit. I think we need to be intentional about how we integrate technology into our lives and societies.
Question 3
How should governments regulate emerging technologies?
Model Answer:
I think regulation needs to be principles-based rather than technology-specific. Technologies evolve too fast for specific regulations to remain relevant. I think governments should establish clear principles — like transparency, accountability, and fairness — and then apply them to specific technologies as they emerge.
Question 4
Do you think there should be a 'right to disconnect' from technology?
Model Answer:
I think yes, absolutely. The expectation that people should be available 24/7 is unhealthy and unsustainable. I think people have a right to their personal time without digital intrusion. Some countries have already introduced legislation around this, and I think it should become universal.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers with specific examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use chronological structure and all cue card prompts
  • ✓ Part 3: Discuss multiple perspectives before stating your position
  • ✓ C1 tip: Use sophisticated, nuanced language with hedging and qualification
