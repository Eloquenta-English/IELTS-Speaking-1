Debate & Argumentation

IELTS Speaking — C1

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
Do you enjoy debating controversial topics?
Model Answer:
I do, actually. I find it intellectually stimulating to examine issues from multiple perspectives. The key is approaching debates with genuine curiosity rather than just trying to win. I think the best debates are where both sides learn something, even if they don't change their minds.
Question 2
Do you think people are becoming better or worse at having respectful discussions?
Model Answer:
I think in some ways we're getting worse. Social media encourages quick, emotional responses rather than thoughtful engagement. People often talk past each other rather than engaging with the actual arguments. But I also think there's growing awareness of this problem, which gives me hope.
Question 3
What skills do you think are most important for effective argumentation?
Model Answer:
I think the ability to understand the other person's perspective is crucial. You can't effectively counter an argument you don't understand. Beyond that, logical reasoning, evidence evaluation, and clear communication are essential. I also think emotional intelligence matters — knowing when to push and when to listen.
Question 4
Should children be taught debating skills in school?
Model Answer:
Absolutely. I think debating teaches critical thinking, research skills, and the ability to articulate ideas clearly. It also teaches children that it's okay to disagree respectfully. I think these are essential skills for citizenship in a democratic society.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Controversial Topic

You should say:

  • What the topic is
  • Why it is controversial
  • What one side argues
  • And explain what the other side argues and which you find more convincing
Model Answer:
A controversial topic I find fascinating is genetic engineering — specifically, the idea of 'designer babies.' On one hand, parents could eliminate genetic diseases and give their children advantages. This seems like a positive use of technology. On the other hand, there are profound ethical concerns. It could create genetic inequality — only wealthy parents could afford enhancements. It also raises questions about consent (the child can't consent to being genetically modified) and about what we consider 'normal' versus 'enhanced.' I find the anti-enhancement argument more convincing. While eliminating disease seems ethically clear, enhancement opens a Pandora's box of social inequality and philosophical questions about human nature. I think the line between therapy and enhancement is too blurry to regulate effectively.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view.

Question 1
Do you think social media has made public discourse better or worse?
Model Answer:
I think it's made it worse in many ways. The algorithms prioritise engagement over accuracy, which means extreme and misleading content spreads faster. But I also think social media has given voice to people who were previously excluded from public debate. The challenge is designing platforms that promote quality discourse.
Question 2
Should there be limits on free speech?
Model Answer:
I think there need to be some limits — things like incitement to violence, defamation, and hate speech should be restricted. But I also think limits should be minimal and carefully defined. The danger of broad speech restrictions is that they can be used to silence legitimate dissent. I think the default should be free expression, with narrow, well-justified exceptions.
Question 3
How do you think political polarisation can be addressed?
Model Answer:
I think it requires action at multiple levels. Education can teach critical thinking and media literacy. Social media platforms can redesign algorithms to reduce echo chambers. But I also think it requires a cultural shift — valuing nuance and complexity over simple answers. I think this is one of the defining challenges of our time.
Question 4
Do you think compromise is a sign of weakness or strength?
Model Answer:
I think it's a sign of strength, but I understand why people see it differently. Genuine compromise requires understanding, flexibility, and the ability to prioritise what matters most. The alternative — refusing to compromise — often leads to worse outcomes for everyone. I think the strongest leaders are those who can find solutions that address everyone's core concerns.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers with specific examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use chronological structure and all cue card prompts
  • ✓ Part 3: Discuss multiple perspectives before stating your position
  • ✓ C1 tip: Use sophisticated, nuanced language with hedging and qualification
