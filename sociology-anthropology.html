Sociology & Anthropology

IELTS Speaking — C1

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
How has family structure changed in your country?
Model Answer:
The traditional nuclear family has become much more diverse. Single-parent families, blended families, same-sex parents, and people choosing to live alone are all much more common than a generation ago. I think this reflects broader social changes — greater individualism, women's increased economic independence, and changing attitudes toward marriage.
Question 2
Do you think communities are stronger or weaker than they used to be?
Model Answer:
I think traditional communities based on geography have weakened. People move more, work more, and have less time for local community activities. But I think new forms of community have emerged — online communities, interest-based groups, and chosen families. The nature of community has changed more than its strength.
Question 3
What do you think is the biggest social problem in your country?
Model Answer:
I think social isolation, particularly among elderly people, is a significant problem. Despite being materially comfortable, many older people are lonely and disconnected. I think this is partly because families are more dispersed than they used to be, and partly because we've lost some of the social structures that used to bring people together.
Question 4
How do you think gender roles have changed in your society?
Model Answer:
There's been enormous progress toward gender equality, but I think significant gaps remain. Women are now well-represented in education and many professions, but there are still disparities in pay, leadership positions, and the distribution of domestic work. I think the next frontier is changing cultural expectations rather than just legal frameworks.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Social Change

You should say:

  • What the change is
  • When it began
  • What caused it
  • And explain how it has affected society
Model Answer:
A significant social change I've observed is the increasing acceptance of mental health discussions. When I was growing up, mental health was rarely discussed openly. People were expected to just 'get on with it.' This change began gradually in the early 2000s, accelerated by social media in the 2010s, and really took off during the pandemic. Celebrities and public figures speaking openly about their mental health struggles helped normalise the conversation. The effect on society has been largely positive. More people seek help, workplaces are more supportive, and there's less stigma. But I also think there's a risk of over-medicalising normal human emotions. The challenge is maintaining support for those with clinical conditions while not pathologising everyday stress.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view.

Question 1
Do you think individual freedom should be limited for the collective good?
Model Answer:
I think there are legitimate cases where individual freedom should be limited — public health measures, environmental regulations, harm to others. But I think any limitation needs to be proportionate, evidence-based, and subject to democratic oversight. The danger is that 'collective good' can be used to justify excessive control.
Question 2
How do you think social media has affected social movements?
Model Answer:
I think social media has made it easier to organise and amplify movements. It allows information to spread rapidly and gives voice to marginalised groups. But I think it also creates 'slacktivism' — people feel they've contributed by sharing a post without taking real action. The challenge is converting online engagement into meaningful change.
Question 3
Do you think cultural traditions are worth preserving even if they conflict with modern values?
Model Answer:
I think this requires case-by-case analysis. Some traditions embody wisdom and community values worth maintaining. Others may conflict with fundamental rights and should be reformed. I think the key is distinguishing between traditions that enrich society and those that simply persist through inertia.
Question 4
How do you think societies will change in the next fifty years?
Model Answer:
I think we'll see significant demographic shifts — aging populations in developed countries, continued urbanisation, and possibly climate migration. I think the nature of work and community will continue to evolve with technology. But I also think fundamental human needs — for connection, meaning, and belonging — will remain constant.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers with specific examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use chronological structure and all cue card prompts
  • ✓ Part 3: Discuss multiple perspectives before stating your position
  • ✓ C1 tip: Use sophisticated, nuanced language with hedging and qualification
