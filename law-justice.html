Law & Justice

IELTS Speaking — C1

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
Do you think the legal system in your country is fair?
Model Answer:
I think it's generally fair but imperfect. The principle of equality before the law is well-established, but in practice, access to justice depends heavily on wealth. Good lawyers are expensive, and legal aid is underfunded. I think this creates a two-tier system where wealthy people have significant advantages.
Question 2
Should prisons focus more on punishment or rehabilitation?
Model Answer:
I think rehabilitation should be the primary focus. The evidence shows that punishment alone doesn't reduce reoffending. Countries that invest in education, job training, and mental health support in prisons have lower recidivism rates. I think the goal should be preparing people to reintegrate into society as productive citizens.
Question 3
Do you think there should be limits on how long people can be imprisoned?
Model Answer:
I think it depends on the crime. For most offenses, long sentences are counterproductive. But for the most serious crimes — things like genocide or mass murder — I think life sentences are appropriate. I think the key is ensuring sentences are proportionate and that there's a pathway to release for those who demonstrate rehabilitation.
Question 4
How has technology changed the legal system?
Model Answer:
Technology has transformed many aspects of law. Digital evidence, online dispute resolution, and legal research tools have made the system more efficient. But there are concerns — algorithmic bias in sentencing, the use of facial recognition by police, and the challenge of regulating new technologies. I think the legal system needs to adapt faster to technological change.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Legal Issue

You should say:

  • What the issue is
  • Why it is controversial
  • What the current law says
  • And explain what you think the law should say
Model Answer:
A legal issue I find fascinating is the question of whether individuals have a 'right to be forgotten' online. This became prominent when a Spanish man sued Google to have search results about his past debts removed. The issue is controversial because it pits freedom of expression and public access to information against individual privacy and the possibility of rehabilitation. The current law in the EU allows people to request removal of certain search results, but the criteria are vague and inconsistently applied. I think the law should allow removal of information that is outdated, irrelevant, or disproportionately harmful to the individual. But it should not allow removal of information about public figures or matters of public interest. The challenge is drawing this line clearly.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view.

Question 1
Should judges be elected or appointed?
Model Answer:
I think appointment is generally better for ensuring judicial independence. Elected judges may be influenced by public opinion or campaign donors. But I think appointments need to be transparent and involve multiple stakeholders — not just political actors. I think the best system involves an independent commission that evaluates candidates based on merit.
Question 2
Do you think international courts are effective?
Model Answer:
I think they're important but limited. The International Criminal Court has brought some war criminals to justice, but enforcement depends on state cooperation. Powerful nations can effectively ignore international courts. I think they need stronger enforcement mechanisms and broader jurisdiction to be truly effective.
Question 3
Should there be a maximum amount of time someone can be held before trial?
Model Answer:
Yes, I think prolonged pretrial detention is a serious injustice. People are innocent until proven guilty, and being imprisoned before trial can destroy lives and livelihoods. I think there should be strict time limits, with exceptions only for the most serious cases where there's a genuine flight risk or danger to others.
Question 4
How do you think artificial intelligence will affect the legal profession?
Model Answer:
I think AI will automate many routine legal tasks — document review, contract analysis, legal research. This could make legal services more affordable and accessible. But I think the judgment, advocacy, and empathy required for lawyering will remain human. I think the legal profession will be transformed but not replaced.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers with specific examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use chronological structure and all cue card prompts
  • ✓ Part 3: Discuss multiple perspectives before stating your position
  • ✓ C1 tip: Use sophisticated, nuanced language with hedging and qualification
