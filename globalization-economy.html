Globalization & Economy

IELTS Speaking — B2

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
How has globalisation affected your country?
Model Answer:
Globalisation has brought many benefits to Austria — access to international markets, cultural exchange, and economic growth. But it's also created challenges. Local businesses struggle to compete with multinational corporations, and there's a sense that some communities have been left behind.
Question 2
Do you think international trade is good for your country's economy?
Model Answer:
Overall, yes. Austria is a small country that benefits enormously from access to international markets. Our economy relies heavily on exports and tourism. But I think the benefits of trade need to be more evenly distributed. Not everyone has gained equally from globalisation.
Question 3
What do you think about the trend of companies moving jobs overseas?
Model Answer:
I understand why companies do it — lower costs, access to new markets. But I think it's harmful to the communities that lose those jobs. I think governments need to invest in retraining and education to help workers adapt. The solution isn't to stop globalisation, but to manage its effects better.
Question 4
Has your own economic situation changed in recent years?
Model Answer:
Like many people my age, I've felt the effects of rising costs — particularly housing and energy. Salaries haven't kept pace with inflation, which makes it harder to save. I think this is a widespread concern, and I'm not sure the government has adequate solutions yet.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

An Economic Change

You should say:

  • What the change was
  • When it happened
  • How it affected people in your country
  • And explain whether you think it was a positive or negative change
Model Answer:
One significant economic change in Austria has been the shift towards renewable energy and the associated transformation of the job market. Over the past decade, there's been massive investment in solar, wind, and hydroelectric power. This change accelerated around 2015 when the government set ambitious climate targets. It's created thousands of new jobs in green technology, but it's also meant job losses in traditional energy sectors like coal. I think overall it's been positive. Austria has positioned itself as a leader in green technology, and the new jobs tend to be higher-skilled and better-paid. But the transition hasn't been smooth for everyone, and some regions have been hit harder than others.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view.

Question 1
Do you think economic growth should be the main goal of a government?
Model Answer:
I think it's an important goal, but not the only one. I think we need to look at broader measures of wellbeing — things like health, education, environmental quality, and happiness. GDP growth doesn't necessarily mean people's lives are improving. I think a more holistic approach to progress is needed.
Question 2
Should wealthy countries do more to help developing nations?
Model Answer:
I think they have a moral responsibility to help, but the form that help matters. I think direct aid can sometimes create dependency. I think fair trade agreements, technology transfer, and investment in education and infrastructure are more effective than just sending money.
Question 3
How do you think the global economy will change in the next twenty years?
Model Answer:
I think we'll see a shift in economic power towards Asia. Climate change will reshape industries — some will disappear, others will emerge. I think automation will continue to transform work. The countries that invest in education and innovation will thrive, while those that don't will struggle.
Question 4
Do you think inequality is an inevitable consequence of economic growth?
Model Answer:
I don't think it's inevitable, but it's common. The benefits of growth tend to flow to those who already have capital and skills. I think progressive taxation, investment in education, and strong social safety nets can reduce inequality. It requires deliberate policy choices from governments.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers with specific examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use chronological structure and all cue card prompts
  • ✓ Part 3: Discuss multiple perspectives before stating your position
  • ✓ B2 tip: Use sophisticated vocabulary but keep it natural
