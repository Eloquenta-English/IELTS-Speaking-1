Media & Communication

IELTS Speaking — B2

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
How do you usually get your news?
Model Answer:
I mostly get my news online — through news websites and social media. I try to use a variety of sources to avoid echo chambers. I also listen to podcasts, which often provide more in-depth analysis than traditional news. I rarely watch TV news anymore.
Question 2
Do you trust the news media in your country?
Model Answer:
I think trust in media has declined significantly, and I understand why. There's a lot of sensationalism and bias, and it can be hard to distinguish between news and opinion. I try to be critical about what I consume and cross-reference stories with multiple sources.
Question 3
Has social media changed the way people communicate in your country?
Model Answer:
Absolutely. It's made communication faster and more convenient, but I think it's also made it more superficial. People are more connected than ever, but many report feeling lonelier. I think the quality of online communication is often lower than face-to-face conversation.
Question 4
Do you think people spend too much time on social media?
Model Answer:
I think many people do. The platforms are designed to be addictive — the infinite scroll, the notifications, the dopamine hits from likes. I've definitely caught myself scrolling mindlessly when I should be doing something more productive. I think it's important to be intentional about social media use.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A News Story

You should say:

  • What the story was about
  • How you learned about it
  • Why it was significant
  • And explain how it made you feel
Model Answer:
A news story that really affected me was the coverage of the refugee crisis in 2015. I remember seeing the images of people fleeing conflict, and particularly the photograph of a young child washed up on a beach in Turkey. I learned about it through multiple channels — traditional news coverage, social media discussions, and conversations with friends. What made it significant was the scale of the humanitarian crisis and the political debates it sparked across Europe. It made me feel a mixture of sadness and frustration. Sadness for the people affected, and frustration at the political responses. It also made me think about privilege — how random the circumstances of where we're born are.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view.

Question 1
Do you think traditional newspapers have a future?
Model Answer:
I think they'll survive in some form, but they'll need to adapt. The days of people buying a physical paper every morning are numbered. I think successful newspapers will focus on in-depth analysis and investigative journalism — things that social media can't replicate.
Question 2
Should social media companies be held responsible for misinformation?
Model Answer:
I think they have some responsibility, but it's a complex issue. They're platforms, not publishers. However, their algorithms actively promote engagement over accuracy, which contributes to the spread of misinformation. I think transparency about how algorithms work and accountability for the consequences is reasonable.
Question 3
How has the 24-hour news cycle affected journalism?
Model Answer:
I think it's had a negative impact on quality. The pressure to be first with a story often means accuracy suffers. There's less time for fact-checking and investigation. I think there's a growing appetite for slower, more thoughtful journalism — the success of long-form podcasts and newsletters suggests this.
Question 4
Do you think people will become better at identifying fake news in the future?
Model Answer:
I'm cautiously optimistic. Media literacy education is improving, and people are becoming more aware of the problem. But the technology for creating fake content is also improving — deepfakes are a growing concern. I think it will be an ongoing arms race.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers with specific examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use chronological structure and all cue card prompts
  • ✓ Part 3: Discuss multiple perspectives before stating your position
  • ✓ B2 tip: Use sophisticated vocabulary but keep it natural
