Culture & Traditions

IELTS Speaking — B2

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics: hobbies, daily life, free time activities.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
What traditions are important in your culture?
Model Answer:
One of the most important traditions in my culture is the celebration of the New Year. It's a time when families gather, share meals, and make resolutions for the year ahead. What makes it special is the symbolism — we burn the old to make way for the new, and there's a real sense of renewal and hope.
Question 2
How have traditions changed in your country over the past fifty years?
Model Answer:
There's been a dramatic shift. Many traditions that were central to daily life have faded or been commercialised. For example, traditional festivals that once involved entire communities are now often reduced to tourist attractions. However, I think there's also been a revival of interest among younger people who want to reconnect with their heritage.
Question 3
Do you think globalisation is erasing cultural differences?
Model Answer:
I think it's a double-edged sword. On one hand, globalisation does tend to homogenise cultures — you see the same brands, the same music, and the same fashion everywhere. But on the other hand, I think it's also creating new forms of cultural exchange and hybrid identities. People are blending traditions from different cultures in creative ways.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future. Don't memorise scripted answers — examiners can tell.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Cultural Event

Describe a cultural event you attended. You should say:

  • What the event was
  • When it took place
  • Who attended
  • And explain why it was significant
Model Answer:
A hobby I really enjoy is playing the guitar. I started learning when I was about 14 — my dad had an old acoustic guitar in the garage and I picked it up one day and taught myself a few chords from YouTube videos.

These days, I try to practise for at least 30 minutes every evening, and on weekends I sometimes play for two or three hours with a friend who also plays.

The reason I enjoy it so much is that it's incredibly creative and relaxing at the same time. When I'm playing, I completely switch off from stress — it's almost meditative. Plus, there's a real sense of achievement when you finally nail a song you've been practising for weeks.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions related to the Part 2 topic.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view. Use phrases like "On one hand... on the other hand..." or "Some people argue that... but I believe..."

Question 1
Why do some people feel a stronger connection to their culture than others?
Model Answer:
I think it varies enormously. Some people grow up immersed in cultural practices — their family speaks their native language at home, celebrates traditional festivals, and maintains customs. Others grow up in more multicultural or globalised environments where cultural identity is less prominent. I think education and personal curiosity also play a role — some people actively seek to understand and preserve their culture, while others are more focused on fitting in.
Question 2
Do you think governments should do more to preserve cultural heritage?
Model Answer:
I think governments have a responsibility to preserve cultural heritage, but it needs to be done thoughtfully. Funding museums, protecting historical sites, and supporting traditional arts are all important. But preservation shouldn't mean freezing culture in time — cultures naturally evolve. I think the best approach is documentation and education — recording traditions, teaching them in schools, and creating spaces where they can be practised, while allowing them to adapt to modern life.
Question 3
How has technology helped or harmed cultural preservation?
Model Answer:
Technology has been both a blessing and a curse. On the positive side, it's made preservation much easier — digital archives, virtual museums, and social media allow cultures to be documented and shared globally. Endangered languages can be recorded, and traditional crafts can find new markets. On the negative side, the internet also accelerates cultural homogenisation. When everyone is consuming the same global content, local traditions can feel less relevant or exciting by comparison.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers — give reasons + examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use all cue card prompts, structure chronologically
  • ✓ Part 3: Balance both sides before giving your opinion
  • ✓ Golden rule: Be specific and personal — avoid generic answers
