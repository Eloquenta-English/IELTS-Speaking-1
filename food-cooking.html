Food & Cooking

IELTS Speaking — B1

Practice all three parts

Question 1
What's your favourite food?
Model Answer:
My favourite food is definitely Wiener Schnitzel — it's a traditional Austrian dish made from veal that's breaded and fried until it's golden and crispy. We usually eat it with potato salad or fries. It reminds me of home because my grandmother used to make it every Sunday for the whole family.
Question 2
Do you know how to cook? What dish can you make?
Model Answer:
Yes, I'd say I'm a decent cook! I can make quite a few dishes now — I taught myself mostly from YouTube videos and by watching my parents. My signature dish is probably pasta carbonara — it's simple but you need good timing to get the egg mixture right without scrambling it. I also make a pretty good apple strudel, which is another Austrian classic.
Question 3
Do you prefer eating at home or in a restaurant?
Model Answer:
It depends on the occasion. For everyday meals, I definitely prefer eating at home because it's healthier and cheaper — I know exactly what's going into the food. But for special occasions or when I want to try something new, going to a restaurant is great. I think restaurants offer experiences that are hard to replicate at home — the atmosphere, the presentation, and the chance to try cuisines from around the world.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future. Don't memorise scripted answers — examiners can tell.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Special Meal You Remember

Describe a special meal you remember. You should say:

  • What the meal was
  • Where you had it
  • Who you were with
  • Why it was special
Model Answer:
I'd like to talk about a meal I had last summer at a traditional Heuriger in the Austrian countryside — that's a type of wine tavern you find mainly in Lower Austria. The meal was Tafelspitz, which is boiled beef served with root vegetables, horseradish, and apple sauce.

I was there with my extended family — my parents, my uncle and aunt, and two cousins — for my grandmother's 80th birthday. The Heuriger was surrounded by vineyards, and we sat outside under the trees with a beautiful view of the rolling hills.

What made it so special was the atmosphere and the company. My grandmother told stories from her childhood, and we all laughed and shared memories. The food was incredible too — simple but perfectly prepared. It wasn't fancy, but it was authentic and full of love. That's what makes a meal truly memorable for me — not the price or the location, but the people you share it with.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions related to the Part 2 topic.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view. Use phrases like "On one hand... on the other hand..." or "Some people argue that... but I believe..."

Question 1
Why do you think food is such an important part of culture?
Model Answer:
Food is central to culture because it brings people together in a way that nothing else can. Think about it — every culture has its own traditional dishes, and these recipes are passed down through generations. Food is how we celebrate, how we comfort each other, and how we express identity. In Austria, for example, our food reflects our history — dishes like Schnitzel and Sachertorte tell a story about who we are and where we come from.
Question 2
Is traditional food in your country changing? How?
Model Answer:
Yes, definitely. I think Austrian cuisine is evolving in interesting ways. On one hand, traditional dishes are still very popular — you can't walk through Vienna without seeing Schnitzel or Sachertzel on every menu. But on the other hand, there's been a huge rise in international cuisines — Thai, Japanese, Middle Eastern — and also modern fusion cooking. Young chefs are taking traditional Austrian ingredients and giving them a contemporary twist. I think it's a positive development, as long as we don't lose our culinary heritage entirely.
Question 3
Do you think people will eat more processed food in the future?
Model Answer:
Unfortunately, I think that's already happening and it will probably continue. Processed food is convenient and cheap, which appeals to people with busy lifestyles. However, I'm hopeful that there will also be a counter-movement — more and more people are becoming aware of the health implications and are choosing organic, local, and seasonal produce. In Austria, we have a strong tradition of quality food, and I think younger generations are becoming more conscious about what they eat. So I think we'll see both trends: more processed food for convenience, but also a growing demand for fresh, high-quality options.
