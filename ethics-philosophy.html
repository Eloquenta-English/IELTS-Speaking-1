Ethics & Philosophy

IELTS Speaking — C1

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
Do you think people are generally becoming more or less ethical?
Model Answer:
I think it depends on what you mean by ethical. In some ways, society has become more ethical — we're more aware of issues like inequality and environmental destruction. But in other ways, the pressure to succeed can lead people to compromise their values. I think awareness has increased, but behaviour hasn't always caught up.
Question 2
Should children be taught ethics in school?
Model Answer:
Absolutely. I think moral reasoning is a skill that needs to be developed, just like reading or mathematics. Children need frameworks for thinking about right and wrong, and they need to learn to consider the consequences of their actions. I think it's one of the most important things schools can teach.
Question 3
Do you think it's ever acceptable to break the law for a good cause?
Model Answer:
This is a classic ethical dilemma. I think there are extreme circumstances — like civil disobedience against unjust laws — where it might be justified. But I also think we need to be very careful about who decides what constitutes a 'good cause.' Generally, I think the social contract requires us to follow laws, even when we disagree with them, while working to change them through democratic means.
Question 4
How do you think technology has changed ethical discussions?
Model Answer:
Technology has created entirely new ethical questions. Things like data privacy, algorithmic bias, and autonomous weapons didn't exist a few decades ago. I think it's made ethics more relevant and urgent. But I also think it's made ethical discussions more complex, because the consequences of technological choices can be far-reaching and hard to predict.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

An Ethical Dilemma

You should say:

  • What the situation was
  • What the dilemma involved
  • What you decided to do
  • And explain whether you think you made the right choice
Model Answer:
An ethical dilemma I faced was discovering that a colleague was taking credit for someone else's work. I was in a meeting where a team member presented another person's analysis as their own. The person whose work was stolen was junior and hadn't been in the room. The dilemma was whether to speak up. On one hand, it wasn't technically my business, and getting involved could create conflict. On the other hand, I felt a responsibility to ensure fairness, especially since the person whose work was stolen was in a more vulnerable position. I decided to speak to my colleague privately after the meeting. I explained that I knew the work wasn't theirs and suggested they correct the record. They were initially defensive but eventually agreed. I think I made the right choice, though it was uncomfortable at the time.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view.

Question 1
Do you think there are universal moral principles, or is morality relative?
Model Answer:
I think there are some universal principles — things like not harming the innocent, keeping promises, and treating people fairly seem to appear across cultures. But I also think the application of these principles varies enormously depending on context. I think the truth is somewhere between absolute moral rules and complete relativism.
Question 2
Should scientists be allowed to research anything, regardless of ethical concerns?
Model Answer:
I think there should be limits. Scientific freedom is important, but not absolute. Research that could cause significant harm — like biological weapons or experiments on non-consenting humans — should be restricted. I think the scientific community needs robust ethical review processes, and society should have a voice in setting boundaries.
Question 3
How do you think artificial intelligence will affect ethical decision-making?
Model Answer:
I think AI will both help and complicate ethics. It can process more information and identify patterns humans might miss, which could improve decisions. But algorithms can also encode biases and make decisions without transparency. I think we need to ensure that AI assists human ethical reasoning rather than replacing it.
Question 4
Do you think people today are more or less tolerant than previous generations?
Model Answer:
I think in many ways we're more tolerant — acceptance of diversity has increased significantly in most societies. But I also think there are new forms of intolerance emerging, particularly online. Social media can create echo chambers where people are less exposed to different viewpoints. I think genuine tolerance requires active effort.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers with specific examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use chronological structure and all cue card prompts
  • ✓ Part 3: Discuss multiple perspectives before stating your position
  • ✓ C1 tip: Use sophisticated, nuanced language with hedging and qualification
