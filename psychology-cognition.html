Psychology & Cognition

IELTS Speaking — C1

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
Why do you think some people are more motivated than others?
Model Answer:
I think motivation is a combination of internal and external factors. Internal factors include personality, values, and intrinsic interest. External factors include incentives, social pressure, and environmental design. I think the most powerful motivation is intrinsic — when people genuinely enjoy what they're doing or believe it's meaningful.
Question 2
Do you think intelligence is primarily genetic or environmental?
Model Answer:
I think it's a complex interaction. Genetic factors set a range, but where someone falls within that range depends enormously on environment — education, nutrition, stimulation, and emotional support. I think the nature versus nurture debate has been largely resolved: both matter, and they interact.
Question 3
How has understanding of mental health changed in recent years?
Model Answer:
The change has been remarkable. Mental health has moved from a taboo subject to something discussed openly in media, workplaces, and schools. We understand much more about the biological, psychological, and social factors involved. I think we're moving toward a more holistic and compassionate approach.
Question 4
Do you think people are generally rational or emotional in their decision-making?
Model Answer:
I think most decisions are primarily emotional, with rationality serving to justify choices after the fact. This is well-documented in behavioural economics. I think this isn't necessarily bad — emotions provide valuable information about what we want and need. But I think awareness of cognitive biases can help us make better decisions.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Psychological Phenomenon

You should say:

  • What the phenomenon is
  • Why it occurs
  • How it affects people
  • And explain whether you think it is generally helpful or harmful
Model Answer:
A psychological phenomenon I find fascinating is the 'bystander effect' — the tendency for people to be less likely to help someone in need when others are present. It was first documented after the 1964 murder of Kitty Genovese in New York, where numerous witnesses failed to intervene. The phenomenon occurs due to diffusion of responsibility (assuming someone else will help) and social influence (looking to others for cues about how to behave). It affects people by making them less likely to notice emergencies, less likely to interpret situations as requiring action, and less likely to take responsibility. I think it's generally harmful, but understanding it can help us overcome it. Simply being aware of the bystander effect makes people more likely to take action. I think this is a good example of how psychological knowledge can be practically applied.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view.

Question 1
Should psychological therapy be more widely available?
Model Answer:
Yes, absolutely. I think access to mental health support should be as normal and accessible as physical health care. The stigma around therapy is decreasing, but cost and availability remain barriers. I think making therapy available through public health systems would have significant social benefits.
Question 2
Do you think social media companies should be required to consider psychological effects of their products?
Model Answer:
I think yes. When you design platforms that billions of people use, you have a responsibility to consider the psychological impact. Features like infinite scroll, variable rewards, and social comparison are deliberately designed to be addictive. I think regulation requiring psychological impact assessments would be appropriate.
Question 3
How do you think our understanding of consciousness will evolve?
Model Answer:
I think we're at the beginning of a revolution in understanding consciousness. Advances in neuroscience, AI, and philosophy are converging to tackle this fundamental question. I think we'll develop better frameworks for understanding consciousness, though whether we'll fully explain subjective experience remains an open question.
Question 4
Do you think there are ethical limits to psychological research?
Model Answer:
Yes, definitely. Research involving deception, harm to participants, or vulnerable populations needs strict ethical oversight. The history of psychology includes too many examples of unethical experiments. I think robust ethical review processes and informed consent are essential, even if they slow down research progress.
