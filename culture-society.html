Culture & Society

IELTS Speaking — B2

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
What do you think are the most important traditions in your country?
Model Answer:
I think the most important ones are related to family gatherings and seasonal celebrations. For example, in Austria we have very strong Christmas traditions that bring the whole family together. These traditions give people a sense of belonging and continuity.
Question 2
How has your culture changed in recent years?
Model Answer:
I think there's been a significant shift towards more individualism, especially among younger people. Traditional community structures like extended family living together have become less common. On the positive side, I think we've become more open and tolerant as a society.
Question 3
Do you think globalisation is affecting local cultures?
Model Answer:
Yes, definitely. You can see it everywhere — the same coffee shops and clothing brands in every city. But I don't think it's all negative. I think there's also a growing appreciation for preserving local traditions and languages, almost as a reaction against globalisation.
Question 4
What role does culture play in your daily life?
Model Answer:
Culture influences everything from how I greet people to how I spend my free time. Even small things like when and what I eat are shaped by cultural norms. I think culture is something you don't notice until you're in a different cultural environment.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Cultural Tradition

You should say:

  • What the tradition is
  • When it takes place
  • What people do during it
  • And explain why it is important in your culture
Model Answer:
A cultural tradition that's very important in my country is the Christmarkt — our Christmas market tradition. These markets take place throughout December in every town and city across Austria. The markets feature local crafts, traditional foods like Kaiserschmarrn and Lebkuchen, and of course Glühwein — mulled wine. Families go together in the evening, children look at the lights and stalls, and adults socialise. I think this tradition is important because it brings communities together during the darkest time of year. It's also a way of passing down local crafts and recipes to younger generations. Personally, it's one of the times I feel most connected to my culture.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view.

Question 1
Do you think it's better to live in a multicultural society or a homogeneous one?
Model Answer:
I think a multicultural society is richer, even though it can be more challenging. When you're exposed to different perspectives, it broadens your understanding of the world. The challenge is ensuring that different communities integrate while maintaining their identities.
Question 2
How do you think technology has affected cultural exchange?
Model Answer:
Technology has made cultural exchange much easier. You can watch films from other countries, follow people from different cultures on social media, and even learn languages online. But I think there's a risk that dominant cultures, particularly American culture, become overwhelming online.
Question 3
Do you think older generations and younger generations have different values?
Model Answer:
Yes, definitely. Older generations tend to value stability, tradition, and community more. Younger people often prioritise individual expression and flexibility. I think neither is wrong — it's more about adapting traditions to modern contexts rather than abandoning them.
Question 4
What do you think will happen to local cultures in the future?
Model Answer:
I think we'll see a bifurcation. Some aspects of local culture will fade as globalisation continues, but I also think there will be a strong movement to preserve and celebrate local traditions. The key is finding the balance between being globally connected and locally rooted.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers with specific examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use chronological structure and all cue card prompts
  • ✓ Part 3: Discuss multiple perspectives before stating your position
  • ✓ B2 tip: Use sophisticated vocabulary but keep it natural
