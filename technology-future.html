Technology & The Future

IELTS Speaking — B2

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
How has technology changed the way you work or study?
Model Answer:
Technology has transformed my daily routine completely. I can collaborate with people across the world, access information instantly, and automate repetitive tasks. The shift to remote work during the pandemic accelerated this trend, and I don't think we'll go back to how things were before.
Question 2
Do you think people spend too much time on their phones?
Model Answer:
I think it depends on the person. For many people, phones have become addictive — constantly checking notifications and scrolling through social media. But I also think phones have made life more convenient in many ways. The key is developing a healthy relationship with technology.
Question 3
Are you worried about the impact of artificial intelligence on employment?
Model Answer:
I think AI will definitely change the job market, but I'm not overly worried. History shows that technology tends to create new jobs even as it eliminates old ones. The challenge is ensuring people can retrain and adapt. Jobs that require human creativity, empathy, and complex problem-solving will be harder to automate.
Question 4
What technological development are you most excited about?
Model Answer:
I'm most excited about advances in renewable energy technology. Things like improved battery storage and more efficient solar panels could genuinely help us tackle climate change. I'm also interested in medical technology — the idea of personalised medicine based on your genetic profile is fascinating.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Technological Change

You should say:

  • What the change was
  • When it happened
  • How it affected people's lives
  • And explain whether you think it was a positive or negative change
Model Answer:
One of the most significant technological changes of recent years has been the widespread adoption of smartphones. When I was a teenager, phones were mainly for calls and texts. Now, they're essentially powerful computers that we carry everywhere. The change happened gradually over about a decade, but the pace accelerated with the introduction of app stores and mobile internet. It's affected almost every aspect of life — how we communicate, navigate, shop, and even date. I think it's been largely positive in terms of convenience and access to information. But there are downsides — the constant connectivity can be exhausting, and there are real concerns about privacy and the impact on mental health, particularly for younger users.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view.

Question 1
Do you think technology makes people more or less connected?
Model Answer:
It's a paradox. Technology makes it easier to stay in touch with people far away, but it can also make us less present with the people physically around us. I think the quality of connection matters more than the quantity. A video call with someone on another continent can be meaningful, but scrolling past friends' updates isn't really connecting.
Question 2
Should there be more regulation of technology companies?
Model Answer:
I think so, yes. The current situation where a few enormous companies control most of the digital space is concerning. They have enormous power over what information people see, and their business models based on advertising and data collection create perverse incentives. I think regulation around data privacy and algorithmic transparency is overdue.
Question 3
How do you think technology will change education in the future?
Model Answer:
I think we'll see more personalised learning, where AI adapts to each student's pace and learning style. Virtual and augmented reality could make learning more immersive. But I think the social aspects of education — learning to collaborate, debate, and build relationships — will remain fundamentally human.
Question 4
Do you think there are any jobs that should never be done by machines?
Model Answer:
I think jobs that require genuine human empathy and ethical judgement should remain human. Things like judges, therapists, and carers for the elderly involve complex human understanding that AI can't replicate. I also think creative work — while AI can assist — should have a human at the centre of the creative process.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers with specific examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use chronological structure and all cue card prompts
  • ✓ Part 3: Discuss multiple perspectives before stating your position
  • ✓ B2 tip: Use sophisticated vocabulary but keep it natural
