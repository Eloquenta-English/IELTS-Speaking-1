Music & Entertainment

IELTS Speaking — B1

Practice all three parts

Question 1
What kind of music do you like?
Model Answer:
I mainly listen to indie rock and alternative music. Bands like Arctic Monkeys and The Killers are among my favourites. I also enjoy some electronic music, especially when I'm studying or exercising. I'm not really into mainstream pop, but I appreciate it when a song has good lyrics and a strong melody.
Question 2
Do you play any musical instruments?
Model Answer:
I play the guitar, though I wouldn't say I'm very good. I took lessons for about a year when I was 15, and I can strum a few chords to accompany singing. I'd love to learn the piano too, but I don't have the time or money for lessons right now. Maybe when I go to university.
Question 3
Do you prefer listening to music alone or with others?
Model Answer:
It depends on the context. When I'm studying or relaxing at home, I prefer listening alone with my headphones because I can really focus on the music. But going to concerts or festivals with friends is amazing too — there's something special about thousands of people singing along together. I think both have their place.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future. Don't memorise scripted answers — examiners can tell.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Concert or Music Event

Describe a concert or music event. You should say:

  • What the concert/event was
  • When and where it happened
  • Who you went with
  • Why it was memorable
Model Answer:
I'd like to talk about a concert I went to last summer. It was a music festival called Nova Rock, which takes place every year in June in Burgenland, in the east of Austria. I went with three of my closest friends, and we'd been planning the trip for months.

The festival had multiple stages with different genres, but the main act we wanted to see was an Austrian band called Wanda. They're known for their energetic live shows and catchy lyrics. We got a spot near the front, and the atmosphere was incredible — everyone was singing and dancing.

What made it so memorable was the combination of great music, good friends, and perfect weather. We camped nearby, so after the concerts we'd sit around talking and playing guitar. It felt like a completely different world from my daily routine — no stress, no responsibilities, just pure enjoyment. I think those are the moments that make life special.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions related to the Part 2 topic.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view. Use phrases like "On one hand... on the other hand..." or "Some people argue that... but I believe..."

Question 1
Do you think music can bring people together?
Model Answer:
Absolutely. Music is one of the most powerful tools for connection. Think about it — people who have nothing in common can bond over a shared favourite artist or song. Music festivals, concerts, and even just sharing playlists create a sense of community. In Austria, we have a strong music tradition, from classical composers like Mozart to modern bands, and music is something that unites people across generations.
Question 2
How has the internet changed the way people discover music?
Model Answer:
The internet has completely transformed music discovery. In the past, you'd hear new music from the radio, from friends, or from physical record stores. Now, streaming platforms use algorithms to recommend songs based on your listening habits, and you can discover artists from anywhere in the world instantly. Social media too — a song can go viral on TikTok and become a global hit overnight. The downside is that there's so much music available that it can be overwhelming, and artists can get lost in the noise.
Question 3
Do you think people will still go to live concerts in the future?
Model Answer:
Yes, definitely. I think live concerts offer something that recorded music simply can't replicate — the energy, the atmosphere, the feeling of being part of something bigger. Even though technology allows you to watch concerts online or experience virtual reality performances, I don't think it will replace the physical experience. If anything, I think live events will become even more special and valuable as everything else moves digital. People crave real, shared experiences.
