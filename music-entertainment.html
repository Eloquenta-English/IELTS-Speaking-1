Music & Entertainment

IELTS Speaking — B1

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics: hobbies, daily life, free time activities.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
What kind of music do you like?
Model Answer:
I have quite eclectic taste in music. I love indie rock and folk music — artists like Bon Iver and Fleet Foxes. But I also enjoy electronic music, especially when I'm working or exercising. I think music is one of the most powerful mood enhancers — the right song can completely change how you feel.
Question 2
Do you play any musical instruments?
Model Answer:
I play the guitar, as I mentioned. I started when I was 14 and I've been playing ever since. I'm not a professional by any means, but I can play most popular songs and I enjoy writing my own music sometimes. It's a great creative outlet and a way to express emotions that are hard to put into words.
Question 3
Do you prefer listening to music alone or with others?
Model Answer:
Both have their appeal. When I'm alone, I put on headphones and really immerse myself in the music — I notice details in the production and lyrics that I'd miss otherwise. But sharing music with friends is special too — going to concerts, discovering new artists together, and having that shared emotional experience. I think it depends on my mood.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future. Don't memorise scripted answers — examiners can tell.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Concert or Performance

Describe a concert or live performance you attended. You should say:

  • What kind of performance it was
  • Where it took place
  • Who performed
  • And explain why it was memorable
Model Answer:
A hobby I really enjoy is playing the guitar. I started learning when I was about 14 — my dad had an old acoustic guitar in the garage and I picked it up one day and taught myself a few chords from YouTube videos.

These days, I try to practise for at least 30 minutes every evening, and on weekends I sometimes play for two or three hours with a friend who also plays.

The reason I enjoy it so much is that it's incredibly creative and relaxing at the same time. When I'm playing, I completely switch off from stress — it's almost meditative. Plus, there's a real sense of achievement when you finally nail a song you've been practising for weeks.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions related to the Part 2 topic.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view. Use phrases like "On one hand... on the other hand..." or "Some people argue that... but I believe..."

Question 1
Why do you think live music is so popular despite streaming services?
Model Answer:
I think it's because live music offers something that recordings simply can't replicate. There's an energy and spontaneity to a live performance — the artist might improvise, interact with the crowd, or play a song differently from the studio version. It's a shared experience with the people around you. And there's something about being physically present at a moment that can never be exactly repeated — it becomes a memory you carry with you.
Question 2
Do you think people spend too much time and money on entertainment?
Model Answer:
I think it depends on the individual and their circumstances. For most people, entertainment is a healthy and important part of life — it helps us relax, connect with others, and find joy. But like anything, it can become excessive. If someone is spending beyond their means, neglecting responsibilities, or using entertainment to avoid dealing with problems, then yes, it's too much. Balance is key.
Question 3
How has technology changed the way people discover and listen to music?
Model Answer:
It's been completely revolutionised. Streaming services give you access to virtually every song ever recorded, and algorithms help you discover new music based on your taste. Social media allows artists to connect directly with fans and promote their work independently. You can even create music on a laptop at home. The downside is that it's made music feel more disposable — people skip tracks more quickly and rarely listen to full albums anymore. But overall, I think the accessibility is a huge positive.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers — give reasons + examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use all cue card prompts, structure chronologically
  • ✓ Part 3: Balance both sides before giving your opinion
  • ✓ Golden rule: Be specific and personal — avoid generic answers
