Environment & Climate

IELTS Speaking — B2

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
Are you concerned about climate change?
Model Answer:
Yes, very much so. I think it's probably the most pressing issue facing my generation. We can already see the effects — more extreme weather, changing seasons, and rising sea levels. What concerns me most is that the window for meaningful action is closing rapidly.
Question 2
What do you do in your daily life to help the environment?
Model Answer:
I try to make small changes — using public transport instead of driving, reducing meat consumption, and being careful about recycling. I also try to buy less fast fashion and support local producers. I know these are small actions, but I believe they add up if enough people do them.
Question 3
Do you think individuals or governments should take more responsibility for environmental issues?
Model Answer:
I think both have a role, but ultimately governments have more power to effect change. Individual actions are important, but systemic change — like investing in renewable energy and regulating emissions — requires government intervention. I think individuals should pressure governments to act.
Question 4
How has the environment in your area changed since you were a child?
Model Answer:
The most noticeable change has been warmer summers and less predictable weather. When I was younger, we had proper snowy winters, but now they're much milder. I've also noticed that some bird species that used to be common are now much rarer.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

An Environmental Problem

You should say:

  • What the problem is
  • Where it occurs
  • What causes it
  • And explain what you think could be done about it
Model Answer:
One environmental problem that really concerns me is plastic pollution in our rivers and oceans. It's a global issue, but I've seen it locally — even in Austria, our rivers carry significant amounts of plastic waste to the Black Sea. The main causes are single-use packaging, poor waste management in some regions, and a culture of convenience where people expect everything to be wrapped in plastic. The problem is that plastic doesn't biodegrade — it just breaks into smaller pieces that enter the food chain. I think the solution requires both government action — banning certain single-use plastics and investing in recycling infrastructure — and individual behaviour change. We need to move towards a circular economy where materials are reused rather than discarded.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view.

Question 1
Do you think economic growth and environmental protection are compatible?
Model Answer:
That's a difficult question. Traditionally, economic growth has come at the expense of the environment. But I think it's possible to have sustainable growth — investing in green technology, renewable energy, and efficient transport. It requires a fundamental shift in how we measure progress, beyond just GDP.
Question 2
Should environmental education be compulsory in schools?
Model Answer:
Absolutely. I think if children learn about environmental issues from a young age, they're more likely to make sustainable choices as adults. It's not just about knowledge — it's about developing a sense of responsibility for the planet. Practical activities like gardening and recycling projects would be more effective than just theory.
Question 3
What do you think about the role of technology in solving environmental problems?
Model Answer:
I think technology is essential, but it's not a silver bullet. We need advances in renewable energy storage, carbon capture, and sustainable materials. But technology alone won't solve the problem — we also need political will and changes in consumer behaviour. The danger is relying on future technological fixes instead of acting now.
Question 4
Do you think future generations will judge our generation for its environmental record?
Model Answer:
Yes, I think they will. Future generations will bear the consequences of our inaction — rising sea levels, biodiversity loss, and resource scarcity. I hope they'll also recognise that many people did try to make a difference, but that the systemic changes needed were blocked by short-term economic interests.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers with specific examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use chronological structure and all cue card prompts
  • ✓ Part 3: Discuss multiple perspectives before stating your position
  • ✓ B2 tip: Use sophisticated vocabulary but keep it natural
