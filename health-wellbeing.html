Health & Wellbeing

IELTS Speaking — B2

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
Do you think people today are healthier than in the past?
Model Answer:
It's a mixed picture. On one hand, we have better medical care and more knowledge about nutrition and exercise. On the other hand, modern lifestyles involve more sitting, more processed food, and more stress. I think overall life expectancy has increased, but quality of life in later years may not have improved as much.
Question 2
What do you do to stay healthy?
Model Answer:
I try to exercise regularly — I go hiking at least twice a week and do yoga. I also pay attention to my diet, trying to eat more whole foods and less processed food. But I think mental health is equally important, so I try to manage stress through meditation and making sure I have enough downtime.
Question 3
How important is mental health compared to physical health?
Model Answer:
I think they're equally important, and they're deeply connected. Poor mental health can manifest as physical symptoms, and chronic physical illness can lead to depression. Unfortunately, I think mental health still carries a stigma in many cultures. I'm glad that's starting to change.
Question 4
Do you think the government should do more to promote public health?
Model Answer:
Yes, I think there's a lot more that could be done. Things like making healthy food more affordable, creating more green spaces for exercise, and ensuring mental health services are accessible. I think prevention is much more cost-effective than treatment, so investing in public health campaigns makes sense.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Healthy Habit

You should say:

  • What the habit is
  • When you started it
  • How you maintain it
  • And explain how it has affected your life
Model Answer:
A healthy habit I've developed over the past few years is going for a morning walk before starting work. I started during the pandemic when I was working from home and needed a clear boundary between work and personal time. I try to walk for about 30 minutes, regardless of the weather. I leave my phone at home, which forces me to be present and notice my surroundings. In winter, I dress warmly and enjoy the quiet of early morning. The effect on my life has been remarkable. I feel more focused during the day, my mood is better, and I sleep more soundly. It's also given me time to think without distractions — some of my best ideas come during these walks.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view.

Question 1
Why do you think some people find it difficult to maintain healthy habits?
Model Answer:
I think it's partly because modern life is designed to encourage unhealthy behaviour — processed food is cheap and convenient, and screens keep us sedentary. But I also think habits require willpower, which is a limited resource. I think the key is starting small and building gradually rather than trying to change everything at once.
Question 2
Do you think alternative medicine should be integrated into mainstream healthcare?
Model Answer:
I think some alternative therapies have value — things like acupuncture for pain management, or meditation for stress reduction. But I think they should be subject to the same rigorous testing as conventional medicine. The danger is when people choose unproven alternative treatments over evidence-based ones for serious conditions.
Question 3
How do you think healthcare will change in the future?
Model Answer:
I think we'll see much more personalised medicine, based on genetic profiles and individual health data. Telemedicine will become more common, making healthcare more accessible. I also think there will be a greater emphasis on preventative care and mental health, rather than just treating illness.
Question 4
Do you think there should be a limit on how much governments can interfere with people's health choices?
Model Answer:
That's a difficult balance. I think governments have a role in protecting public health — things like seatbelt laws and smoking bans have clearly saved lives. But there's a line between protecting people and controlling their personal choices. I think the key is education and creating environments where healthy choices are the easy option.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers with specific examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use chronological structure and all cue card prompts
  • ✓ Part 3: Discuss multiple perspectives before stating your position
  • ✓ B2 tip: Use sophisticated vocabulary but keep it natural
