Science & Innovation

IELTS Speaking — B2

Practice all three parts

◆ Part 1 — Interview (4-5 min)

The examiner asks general questions about familiar topics.

Tip: Give extended answers (2-3 sentences), not just "Yes" or "No". Always give a reason or example.

Question 1
Are you interested in science?
Model Answer:
Yes, I find it fascinating, even though I don't have a scientific background. I'm particularly interested in space exploration and medical breakthroughs. I think science has the potential to solve many of humanity's biggest challenges, from climate change to disease.
Question 2
What recent scientific development do you find most exciting?
Model Answer:
I think the development of mRNA vaccines is remarkable. The speed at which they were developed during the pandemic was extraordinary. It's opened up possibilities for treating other diseases, including some types of cancer. I think we're at the beginning of a revolution in medicine.
Question 3
Do you think the government should spend more on scientific research?
Model Answer:
Absolutely. I think investment in research pays dividends in the long run. The internet, GPS, and many medical advances all came from government-funded research. I think private companies tend to focus on short-term profits, so government funding is essential for long-term, fundamental research.
Question 4
How has technology changed the way scientists work?
Model Answer:
Technology has transformed science. Researchers can collaborate globally, access vast databases, and use AI to analyse data. I think the pace of discovery has accelerated dramatically. But I also think there are concerns about reproducibility and the pressure to publish.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Scientific Discovery

You should say:

  • What the discovery was
  • When it was made
  • Who made it
  • And explain why you think it is significant
Model Answer:
A scientific discovery I find particularly significant is the detection of gravitational waves, which was announced in 2016. It was made by the LIGO collaboration — a team of over a thousand scientists from around the world. The discovery confirmed a prediction made by Einstein a century earlier. It's significant because it gives us an entirely new way of observing the universe. Before, we could only 'see' the universe through electromagnetic radiation — light, radio waves, X-rays. Now we can detect ripples in space-time itself. I think it's a perfect example of how fundamental research, driven by curiosity rather than immediate practical goals, can transform our understanding of reality.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view.

Question 1
Should there be limits on scientific research?
Model Answer:
I think there should be ethical limits, yes. Things like human cloning or biological weapons research should be restricted. But I also think that excessive regulation can stifle innovation. The key is having robust ethical frameworks that allow research to proceed while protecting against harmful applications.
Question 2
Do you think people will eventually live on other planets?
Model Answer:
I think it's possible, but probably not in my lifetime. The technical challenges are enormous — radiation, lack of atmosphere, psychological effects of isolation. I think we'll see permanent bases on the Mars eventually, but large-scale colonisation is much further off. I also think we should focus on protecting Earth before looking to other planets.
Question 3
How do you think artificial intelligence will change scientific research?
Model Answer:
I think AI will revolutionise science. It can process vast amounts of data, identify patterns humans might miss, and even generate hypotheses. I think we'll see AI-designed drugs, AI-predicted protein structures, and AI-optimised experiments. But I think human creativity and intuition will remain essential.
Question 4
Do you think everyone should study some science in school?
Model Answer:
Yes, I think basic scientific literacy is essential for everyone, not just future scientists. Understanding how science works helps people make informed decisions about health, technology, and the environment. It also teaches critical thinking and evidence-based reasoning, which are valuable in every field.
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓ Part 1: Extend your answers with specific examples
  • ✓ Part 2: Use chronological structure and all cue card prompts
  • ✓ Part 3: Discuss multiple perspectives before stating your position
  • ✓ B2 tip: Use sophisticated vocabulary but keep it natural
