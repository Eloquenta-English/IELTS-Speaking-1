Health & Fitness

IELTS Speaking — B1

Practice all three parts

Question 1
Do you exercise? What kind of exercise do you do?
Model Answer:
Yes, I try to exercise regularly. I go jogging about three times a week, usually in the park near my house or along the Danube river. In winter, I sometimes go swimming at the local indoor pool. I also do some basic stretching and bodyweight exercises at home when I don't have time to go out.
Question 2
Have your habits changed in the last few years?
Model Answer:
Definitely. A few years ago, I was quite sedentary — I'd spend most of my free time on the computer or watching TV. But then a friend invited me to go hiking with him, and I discovered that I actually enjoyed being active. Since then, I've made a conscious effort to move more, and now I feel much healthier and more energetic.
Question 3
Is it important for children to stay physically active?
Model Answer:
Absolutely. I think it's crucial for children to be active because it builds healthy habits that last a lifetime. Kids who exercise regularly tend to have better concentration in school, stronger immune systems, and higher self-esteem. In Austria, we're lucky because there are lots of opportunities for outdoor activities, but I worry that too many children spend too much time on screens these days instead of playing outside.
◆ Part 2 — Long Turn (3-4 min)

You will receive a cue card with a topic. You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1-2 minutes.

Tip: Use all the prompts on the card. Structure your answer: past → present → future. Don't memorise scripted answers — examiners can tell.

◆ Part 2 — Cue Card

A Time You Were Really Fit or Healthy

Describe a time you were really fit or healthy. You should say:

  • When it was
  • What you were doing
  • How you felt
  • What you learned
Model Answer:
I'd like to talk about a time when I was really fit — about two years ago when I trained for a 10-kilometre charity run. I followed a training programme for about three months, which involved running three times a week, gradually increasing the distance.

On the day of the race, I felt incredibly nervous but also excited. The weather was perfect — cool and sunny — and there were thousands of people running together, which created an amazing atmosphere. I managed to finish in under 55 minutes, which was better than my target time.

What I learned from that experience is that consistency is key. You don't have to be naturally athletic — you just need to show up and put in the effort regularly. It also taught me that exercise is as much mental as physical. When you push through the difficult moments, you discover what you're truly capable of.
◆ Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 min)

The examiner asks more abstract, in-depth questions related to the Part 2 topic.

Tip: Give balanced opinions. Acknowledge both sides before giving your own view. Use phrases like "On one hand... on the other hand..." or "Some people argue that... but I believe..."

Question 1
Why do you think some people find it difficult to stay healthy?
Model Answer:
I think there are several barriers. Firstly, motivation is a big one — it's hard to maintain exercise habits when you don't see immediate results. Secondly, time is a major factor. People who work long hours or have family responsibilities simply may not have the energy or free time to exercise. Finally, access matters — not everyone lives near parks, gyms, or safe outdoor spaces, and gym memberships can be expensive.
Question 2
Do you think the government should do more to promote public health?
Model Answer:
Yes, I think governments have a significant role to play. In Austria, we have good infrastructure for sports and recreation, but I think more could be done, especially in schools. For example, increasing physical education hours, subsidising sports clubs for lower-income families, and running public awareness campaigns about healthy eating. I also think urban planning matters — building more bike lanes and pedestrian zones makes it easier for people to be active as part of their daily routine.
Question 3
How has technology changed the way people exercise?
Model Answer:
Technology has made exercise more accessible and data-driven. Fitness apps and smartwatches allow people to track their workouts, monitor their heart rate, and set goals. There are also countless online workout videos for every fitness level, so you can exercise at home without needing a gym. On the flip side, I think technology can also be a distraction — some people spend more time tracking their exercise than actually exercising. And the rise of sedentary screen-based entertainment is definitely contributing to more inactive lifestyles.
✓ Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Part 3: Balance both sides before giving your opinion
